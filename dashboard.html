Trend

Sales spike every November–December and dip every summer. The seasonal pattern repeats in all three years, with a sharp single-month peak (likely a holiday or year-end procurement cycle). Profit doesn't always track sales 1:1 — several high-sales months show comparatively thin profit, signalling heavier discounting in those periods.

Category Mix

Technology leads total sales, followed by Office Supplies and Furniture. Technology also carries the strongest margins, making it the category most worth protecting from over-discounting.

Geography

East and Central together generate over half of all sales. South is the smallest region — a candidate for either a growth push or a cost review, depending on its margin profile.

Risk Area

Furnishings and Bookcases are the weakest sub-categories — their total profit is barely above zero despite meaningful sales volume. Copiers and Machines, by contrast, are disproportionately profitable for their order count. A targeted pricing or discount-cap review on Furnishings/Bookcases would have the most direct impact on the bottom line.

Root Cause

Margins turn negative once discounts pass roughly 30%. Orders discounted at 30%+ lost a combined $71.5K — discounting policy, not product cost, is the main lever behind the loss-making sub-categories above.

Top Performers

Pennsylvania, New York and Texas anchor national sales. These top states are spread across regions, suggesting performance is driven by individual state demand rather than one region carrying the company.

Summary Storyboard — Three Takeaways for Leadership

  1. Protect Technology, the profit engine. It drives the most sales and the healthiest margins — pricing and inventory decisions should prioritize keeping it well stocked and minimally discounted.
  2. Cap discounts at 20–25%. The data shows a clear break-even point — past it, every additional discount point converts directly into a loss, costing roughly $71.5K across the dataset alone.
  3. Re-evaluate Furnishings and Bookcases. These sub-categories sell consistently but contribute almost nothing to profit; a pricing audit or supplier renegotiation is the highest-leverage fix available in the current portfolio.
